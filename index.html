An independent research lab

I've never met a broken process I didn't want to fix.

A messy workflow at work, a bad system in class, a dumb workaround at home — give me enough time inside anything and I'll start pointing at what could run better. This lab is that instinct, pointed somewhere useful: a running study of where organizations lose the most to fragmented coordination, and how small the fix can actually be.

01 — Why this exists

Coordination fails before expertise does.

The same pattern shows up across healthcare, enterprise software, finance, compliance, and customer operations: information gets fragmented, ownership shifts across teams mid-process, approvals stack up, and context gets lost at every handoff. None of this is a knowledge problem — the people involved usually know exactly what needs to happen. It's a coordination problem, and coordination problems compound: a delay at one handoff doesn't stay contained, it grows by the time it reaches whoever has to act on it next.

AI gets pitched as a way to replace the person doing the work. That's the wrong target here. The better question is whether AI can sit between the people already doing the work — carrying context across the handoff instead of losing it, surfacing the decision instead of burying it in a thread or a form. That's the gap this lab studies.

02 — How I work

The same sequence, every time.

Whether the workflow is a healthcare prior authorization or a contract handoff, every project moves through the same nine steps.

  1. 01Observe the workflow as it actually happens, not as it's documented
  2. 02Map the end-to-end process
  3. 03Talk to the people doing the work
  4. 04Find where the friction actually is, not where it's assumed to be
  5. 05Quantify the business and quality impact
  6. 06Design what the workflow should look like instead
  7. 07Build a working prototype
  8. 08Test it with real users
  9. 09Write down what worked, what didn't, and iterate

03 — The framework

Not every broken process is worth fixing first.

I prioritize on two axes: how much fixing something changes downstream, and how narrow the first fix can actually be.

Impact

Does fixing this change how much else moves? A breakdown that a lot of people and systems depend on matters more than an isolated one. Something that keeps happening matters more than something that happened once.

Resource

How narrow can the first fix be? A single structured output that replaces one manual step is low-resource, even if the process around it is huge. Rebuilding multiple systems is high-resource, no matter the eventual payoff.

I'm looking for the overlap: high impact, low resource — the narrow wedge that moves a disproportionate amount of the system once it's placed right. High impact but high resource is worth watching, not building yet. Low impact usually gets skipped, however easy it'd be.

04 — Active work

Four projects clearing the bar.

Active — proven
Healthcare Prior Authorization

AI Submission Coordinator

The proof case for this whole approach. One structured decision point in a process that otherwise runs on manual review across payer-specific rules — built and tested as a working prototype.

Active — scoping
Customer Operations

Customer Escalation Management

Every escalation is where the cost of every upstream failure shows up at once — it comes down to one decision: escalate or not, and to whom. One system of record, one structured decision, and most orgs already track the cost, which makes this an easier case to build.

Active — scoping
Compliance & Legal

Regulatory & Policy Change Tracking

When a rule changes, most organizations still catch it manually — someone notices, figures out what it touches, and routes it to the right person. This doesn't need access to any one company's internal systems; the underlying regulatory data is public.

Active — scoping
Cross-Functional Ops

Cross-Team SLA & Handoff Breach Detection

Almost every internal handoff — engineering to support, sales to legal, ops to finance — has an informal time expectation that nobody actually tracks. Breaches get caught late, after the damage has already moved downstream. This is the escalation pattern, generalized to any handoff with a clock on it.

Healthcare

Care Coordination

High-impact by nature — multiple providers, systems, and a patient all depend on the same handoffs working. Not scoped to one wedge yet. The full coordination problem is too broad to prototype at once, and figuring out which single handoff to start with is still an open question.

Watching
Enterprise Sales

Autonomous Deal Coordination

Deal execution touches legal, sales, compliance, and finance, but a lot of the friction is judgment-heavy — redlines, pricing exceptions, negotiation — which cuts against keeping humans in the loop where judgment actually matters. Watching this one, not building it yet.
